# **Voice-First Dual-Sided Service Matching: Engineering the LOC8W1 Platform for the AfterMath Hackathon**

The digital revolution has disproportionately favored populations equipped with high literacy rates, robust internet connectivity, and advanced hardware. In stark contrast, hundreds of millions of individuals in emerging economies remain excluded from digital marketplaces due to the rigid, text-heavy nature of contemporary user interfaces. The gig economy, which relies heavily on dynamic matching between service providers and consumers, currently operates behind a formidable barrier of complex application navigation, digital forms, and English-dominant text ecosystems. This structural inequality is the primary problem space targeted by the LOC8W1 platform, a conceptual multilingual voice-assisted job connection platform. The core premise of LOC8W1 acknowledges that millions of India's blue-collar workers still rely on inefficient word-of-mouth networks for employment, while users concurrently struggle to find trusted, available service providers swiftly. Existing digital platforms exhibit severe limitations: they are predominantly English-centric, entirely urban-focused, demand high digital literacy, and require significant typing and screen navigation. Consequently, rural and semi-urban users are severely underserved, despite demonstrating a strong preference for voice interactions in their regional languages and dialects.

To democratize access to these essential economic engines, a paradigm shift is required, moving away from visual, touch-based interfaces toward voice-activated, vernacular-aware systems. The LOC8W1 platform is conceived as a dual-sided voice interaction marketplace designed specifically for India's blue-collar ecosystem. By allowing a customer to simply state a problem in their native dialect—such as "I have a tap problem"—and instantly matching them with the nearest relevant worker, the platform completely eliminates the cognitive and technical friction inherent in traditional applications. Developing this sophisticated platform within the stringent 24-hour constraints of the AfterMath Hackathon requires a highly optimized architectural approach. ## **The Socio-Technical Context and Cognitive Load**

Before architecting the technical solution, it is vital to understand the cognitive limitations and technological constraints of the target demographic. Research into voice user interfaces for illiterate and semi-literate populations reveals that traditional graphical user interfaces demand high cognitive processing, particularly in structural and abstract thinking.2 Users with low digital literacy often avoid complex functions on smartphones, utilizing them almost exclusively for basic voice communication and simple media consumption.3 When interacting with digital systems, these users experience significant friction when forced to navigate hierarchical menus, decipher icons, or type queries.4

The LOC8W1 platform addresses this by completely collapsing the user journey into a single auditory interaction. This approach aligns with ethnographic design studies conducted in developing regions, which confirm that text-free designs heavily reliant on voice feedback are strongly preferred by communities unable to read fluently.6 However, implementing a voice interface for this demographic requires more than simple dictation software. The system must accommodate a lower speech rate, handle non-goal-oriented dialog phrasing, and provide deliberate, appropriately paced auditory feedback.8 Furthermore, the system must navigate the complex linguistic landscape of India, where users frequently engage in code-mixing—blending regional languages with English terminology mid-sentence—and exhibit diverse regional accents.9

The AfterMath hackathon emphasizes building technology that reaches those left behind by the technology revolution.1 By replacing visual navigation with a conversational agent capable of understanding vernacular intent, the LOC8W1 platform transforms a complex dual-sided marketplace into an intuitive, accessible service accessible via a basic smartphone.

## **Architectural Paradigm: The MERN Stack and Real-Time Telemetry**

To facilitate instant voice-based matching, the underlying infrastructure must support continuous, bidirectional data flow with absolute minimal latency. Traditional HTTP request-response cycles are entirely inadequate for live audio streaming, as the overhead of establishing a new connection for every audio chunk would introduce unacceptable delays. Furthermore, HTTP polling is inefficient for alerting a worker the moment a match occurs.11 Consequently, the architecture relies on the MERN stack augmented heavily by WebSockets to create a persistent, full-duplex communication channel between the clients and the server.12

### **Frontend Audio Capture and Processing Strategy**

The client-side interface, built with React, must prioritize extreme simplicity. For users with low digital literacy, the interface should ideally consist of a single, highly visible interaction point—a prominent microphone button—devoid of nested menus, text inputs, or complex onboarding screens.15 When the user activates the microphone, the application must capture acoustic data and transmit it to the backend for real-time analysis.

A critical architectural decision at this juncture involves the format of the audio data. Sarvam AI's streaming WebSocket APIs, which are utilized for the speech processing, strictly require audio to be formatted as WAV or raw PCM (Pulse-Code Modulation) at a precise 16kHz sample rate.16 Because standard browser implementations of the MediaRecorder API often default to compressed formats like WebM or Ogg, the React application requires a specialized approach.18 While libraries like react-media-recorder are popular, they often struggle with direct PCM streaming, necessitating the use of the Web Audio API directly or specialized libraries like RecordRTC.19

The optimal frontend implementation involves utilizing the AudioContext interface to access the microphone stream. A ScriptProcessorNode or a modern AudioWorkletNode is then attached to the stream to intercept the raw floating-point audio samples.18 Because these samples are typically captured at the hardware's native sample rate (often 44.1kHz or 48kHz), the frontend must programmatically downsample the audio to 16kHz. Following downsampling, the 32-bit floating-point values are clamped and converted into 16-bit signed integers to fulfill the raw PCM requirement.18

Once the raw PCM data is properly formatted, it cannot be buffered until the user finishes speaking, as waiting for the end of the sentence would introduce massive latency and destroy the real-time interaction model. Instead, the audio is sliced into continuous, discrete chunks of approximately 250 milliseconds.20 These chunks are serialized into binary blobs or base64 encoded strings and transmitted over a secure WebSocket connection to the Node.js backend.20 This aggressive chunking strategy ensures that the time-to-first-byte (TTFB) for downstream processing remains in the sub-second range, creating an illusion of instantaneous comprehension for the customer.

### **Backend Orchestration and State Management**

The Node.js and Express backend acts as the central nervous system of the LOC8W1 platform. It must handle incoming WebSocket connections from potentially thousands of concurrent users and workers while managing the state of each active service request.12 Upon receiving a WebSocket connection from the React client, the Express server authenticates the user using a lightweight JSON Web Token (JWT) transmitted during the initial handshake, ensuring session security without requiring a complex, visual login flow.12

Libraries such as Socket.io are highly recommended for this implementation. While raw ws provides a closer-to-the-metal implementation, Socket.io provides built-in mechanisms for ping/pong heartbeats, which are absolutely critical for maintaining persistent connections on unstable 4G mobile networks common in rural and semi-urban Indian environments.11 Without proper heartbeat monitoring, mobile connections frequently drop silently, leading to matched jobs being sent to disconnected workers.11

As the binary audio chunks stream into the Node.js server from the customer, they are immediately piped to external AI processing services. The backend must implement robust backpressure handling; if the external AI APIs process data slower than the user speaks, the Node.js server must buffer the chunks gracefully to prevent memory leaks or catastrophic data loss.12 Furthermore, the server maintains an in-memory registry or utilizes a fast key-value store like Redis to map active WebSocket session IDs to user profiles and geographic coordinates.24 This architecture ensures that when the AI extracts the service intent from the voice stream, the server immediately possesses the contextual location data required to initiate the matching algorithm without requiring additional database lookups.

## **Voice Intelligence Pipeline: Advanced Sarvam AI Integration**

The cornerstone of the LOC8W1 platform is its ability to comprehend chaotic, vernacular, and heavily code-mixed audio.10 Indian users rarely speak in pure Hindi, Tamil, or English; they seamlessly blend regional languages with English terminology (e.g., "Mera tap leak kar raha hai"). Traditional global automatic speech recognition models trained primarily on Western datasets struggle profoundly with this acoustic reality, often misinterpreting critical nouns, hallucinating text, or failing entirely in noisy, real-world environments.25

### **Bypassing Cascaded Architectures with End-to-End Speech-to-Command**

Historically, voice assistants and automated platforms have relied on a rigid, cascaded architecture. In this paradigm, a Voice Activity Detection (VAD) module detects speech, an ASR model converts the speech to text, the transcribed text is forwarded to a Large Language Model for intent extraction, and the LLM's text output is finally sent to a Text-to-Speech engine.26 This sequential processing introduces compounding latency at every step, often resulting in response times exceeding several seconds, which effectively destroys the conversational illusion.26 Furthermore, critical acoustic context—such as the user's urgency, frustration, tone, or subtle pauses—is permanently discarded when the rich audio signal is flattened into a one-dimensional text string.26

To optimize for the AfterMath hackathon's requirement of instant utility and real-world deployability, the LOC8W1 backend architecture utilizes Sarvam Audio's end-to-end Speech-to-Command capabilities.26 Sarvam Audio is built upon Sarvam 3B, a 3-billion-parameter foundation language model pre-trained from scratch on English and 22 Indian languages, designed specifically to handle speech as a direct contextual signal rather than a simple transcription task.26 By utilizing this advanced API, the Node.js server bypasses the intermediate transcription step entirely.

The backend implementation involves supplying the Sarvam API with a specific "Tool Definition" representing the LOC8W1 platform's matching logic.26 The server defines a conceptual tool named BookService with required parameters such as intent, problem_category, urgency, and confirmation. When the streaming 16kHz PCM audio chunks reach the Sarvam processing node, the model analyzes the acoustic data directly against this strict tool definition.

If a customer activates the app and says, "Bhaiya, jaldi ek plumber bhejo, bathroom ka tap toot gaya hai," the API does not simply return a transcript. Instead, it evaluates the acoustic nuances, language mixing, and intent, and responds directly with a structured JSON payload indicating the function invocation: Calling tool: BookService("intent": "repair", "problem_category": "plumbing", "urgency": "high").26 This direct function calling drastically reduces system latency, significantly simplifies the Node.js state management architecture, and preserves the semantic intent despite heavy code-mixing or environmental background noise.26 By avoiding multiple LLM reasoning calls and deterministic slot managers, the system avoids conversational feedback loops and rapid token consumption.28

### **Model Selection: Saaras v3 vs. Legacy Systems**

For the implementation of the streaming WebSocket connection, the architecture mandates the use of the saaras:v3 model over the legacy saarika:v2.5 model.16 Saaras v3 is a state-of-the-art model that provides highly accurate transcription across 22 supported Indian languages and features flexible output modes.10 While modes like transcribe or translate are useful for general applications, the LOC8W1 platform relies on the model's inherent ability to process code-mixed speech seamlessly, accurately separating English nouns (like "plumber" or "tap") from the regional verbs and grammar structures.10 The model evaluates Intent Preservation and Entity Preservation far more effectively than baseline models like Gemini-3-Flash, ensuring that critical service details are never lost in translation.26

### **Vernacular Auditory Feedback Generation**

In a low-literacy context, visual confirmations such as a green checkmark, a loading spinner, or a text-based pop-up are entirely insufficient for providing user confidence.30 The user must receive immediate, culturally resonant, and highly intelligible auditory feedback.30 Once the backend successfully parses the intent and successfully locates a worker via the geospatial engine, it must immediately communicate this success to the user.

To achieve this, the Node.js server utilizes Sarvam's Bulbul v3 Text-to-Speech (TTS) model.32 Bulbul v3 is a production-ready synthesis engine supporting 11 Indian languages, offering over 25 distinct speaker voices with customizable parameters for pace and temperature.33 The backend dynamically constructs a response string localized to the user's detected language. For instance, if the matching engine secures a worker, the backend generates the string: "Aapka plumber, Rajesh, paanch minute mein aa raha hai" (Your plumber, Rajesh, is arriving in five minutes).

This dynamic string is transmitted to the Bulbul v3 WebSocket streaming endpoint alongside configuration parameters defining the specific speaker voice (e.g., "shubh"), the target language code (e.g., "hi-IN"), and the desired output audio codec.32 As Sarvam generates the synthesized audio, it streams the resulting binary data back to the Node.js server. The server, acting as a real-time proxy, immediately pipes this audio stream through the established WebSocket connection directly to the user's React frontend for instantaneous playback.32 This intricate choreography creates a seamless, bidirectional conversational loop that requires zero visual reading or physical interaction from the user beyond the initial microphone press.

## **Geospatial Matching and Advanced Worker Routing**

Extracting the user's intent accurately represents only the first phase of the dual-sided transaction. The platform must then identify, qualify, and route the absolute nearest available service provider. This requires a highly efficient, mathematically rigorous spatial querying mechanism capable of operating at scale.

### **MongoDB Spatial Indexing and Query Execution**

As service workers (plumbers, electricians, mechanics) log into the LOC8W1 platform via their respective devices, their applications continuously transmit their GPS coordinates to the Node.js server, which updates their location state in a MongoDB cluster.35 To perform rapid, hyper-local matching, worker coordinates must be stored utilizing specific GeoJSON formatting.36 The database schema dictates an embedded document structure featuring a type field explicitly set to "Point" and a coordinates array strictly ordered as \[longitude, latitude\].36

Crucially, the MongoDB collection housing the worker data must feature a 2dsphere index applied to the location field.36 Unlike basic 2d indexes that calculate flat Euclidean distances, the 2dsphere index forces MongoDB to calculate distances using spherical geometry, accurately accounting for the curvature of the Earth.36 This level of precision is vital for accurate urban matching where minor coordinate discrepancies can result in significant real-world routing errors.

Upon receiving the structured payload from the Sarvam Audio API containing the problem_category (e.g., plumbing), the Node.js server executes a complex aggregation pipeline utilizing the $nearSphere query operator.37 The query is constructed to filter the worker collection for individuals possessing the skill tag matching the problem_category, ensuring their current status is set to available, and sorting them automatically by spherical proximity to the customer's exact coordinates.37 A $maxDistance parameter, measured in meters, is strictly enforced to restrict the search radius to a predefined logical limit (e.g., 5000 meters) to ensure that the platform does not attempt to match a customer with a worker located in a different municipal district.38

### **Validating Commute Times via Third-Party APIs**

While MongoDB's $nearSphere operator provides exceptionally fast straight-line spherical distance calculations, this metric is frequently deceptive in densely populated Indian metropolitan areas. A straight-line distance of two kilometers might necessitate crossing an unnavigable river, an industrial zone, or a heavily congested railway line, resulting in commute times that wildly diverge from the geographic proximity.

To refine the match and guarantee service speed, the backend extracts the top three closest candidates returned by the MongoDB query and dispatches their coordinates to the Google Maps Routes Matrix API.39 The Routes Matrix API computes the actual driving or walking time required to navigate the street network, actively factoring in real-time traffic conditions. By passing the customer's origin point and the three workers' respective destination points, the API returns a matrix of precise Estimated Times of Arrival (ETAs).40

The Node.js server analyzes this matrix and selects the worker boasting the lowest actual ETA, rather than the shortest geographic distance. This supplementary API call introduces a minor latency penalty to the matching algorithm—typically in the realm of 100 to 200 milliseconds—but it exponentially increases the reliability and practical utility of the platform's core value proposition.

## **Omnichannel Worker Notification and Telephony Fallbacks**

A critical failure point in modern gig economy applications is the assumption of perpetual, high-speed connectivity. Many blue-collar gig workers in India rely on budget smartphones and frequently operate in areas with intermittent 4G coverage, or they purposefully disable mobile data to conserve costly prepaid balances.41 Relying solely on internet-based push notifications or persistent WebSockets to alert a worker to a lucrative job opportunity represents a severe architectural vulnerability.

To guarantee that the matched worker receives the job request immediately, the LOC8W1 platform incorporates an omnichannel notification strategy, utilizing Twilio's Programmable Voice and SMS APIs as an unyielding fallback mechanism.43 If the Node.js server attempts to route a match via WebSocket but detects that the worker's connection is dormant, dropped, or fails to acknowledge the ping heartbeat within a specific threshold, it immediately triggers a server-side Twilio API call.11

Twilio initiates a standard cellular phone call to the worker's mobile number. Upon the worker answering, the Twilio system executes a TwiML (Twilio Markup Language) script that plays an automated, vernacular message previously generated by Sarvam's TTS engine: "Aapke paas do kilometer door ek naya plumbing request hai. Accept karne ke liye ek dabayein" (You have a new plumbing request two kilometers away. Press 1 to accept).43 This robust fallback mechanism ensures that the platform functions flawlessly regardless of the worker's immediate digital connectivity status or digital literacy level, directly answering the AfterMath hackathon's mandate to design solutions deployable in realistic, unideal conditions.1
